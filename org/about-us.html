Care for the soil, water and current residents of the Pine Street Barge Canal.

What is the Pine Street Barge Canal?

The Pine Street Barge Canal (PSBC) is 28 acres of undeveloped land in the heart of Burlington’s South End. A former wetland, then an industrial site, built largely on filled land, it is identified as a Superfund site due to toxic waste residues, mostly hydrocarbons and heavy metals. Because it has largely been left alone for 40 years, it is also a functioning ecosystem –  home to many plants (native and non-native), mammals, amphibians, birds, insects, and fungi. Left alone, it has contained the toxins that would otherwise threaten the soil and groundwater while naturally attenuating the toxins through underground network collaborations and nutrient exchange networks.

Who owns the Barge Canal?

The City of Burlington owns 11 acres. The rest is privately owned or is water (state land). Four acres of the private land are currently under contract for development of a nordic spa and bowling alley. If and how these enterprises benefit our community is for the public to decide as the developers move through the permitting process. But the remaining land, if rezoned as conservation land, could become a public resource, a place of learning –  educating and training others how to partner with nature to heal the legacy of our industrial development.

Who are the “Friends of the Barge Canal?”

We are an organization of concerned local residents who have been working since October 2021 to support conservation efforts that are taking place at the Barge Canal. In a sense, we have put down our roots next to the mycelial foundation that feeds the life above. We need your help.

We all need this land left alive – that beautiful network of fungi, bacteria and living roots below that feed the flowers, trees, birds and foxes above. We are learning how to partner with the land as we go, relearning what indigenous people understand, that life depends on this living foundation.

Since the beginning, we have been in contact with local Abenaki leaders and community members, City decision-makers, the private landowners and local non-profits, but our efforts have remained largely independent as the city focuses only on economic redevelopment. In our vision, the Barge Canal is a place to learn. 

Why should I care?

In this time of climate emergency, conserving urban forests, riparian zones, and wetlands is crucially important. It is also our responsibility as inheritors of settler privilege from colonial industrial society to clean up the toxic legacy of those who came before. This means to facilitate innovative implementation of true remediation. This involved updating the archaic definition of remediation (currently used and defined as: paving over, digging up, dumping, and building) to what ture remediation that is practiced in the ecological restoration and scientific communities. This involves actually cleaning up the toxins through working with native microbes, fungi, and plants.

It is also our responsibility as an industrialized society to clean up our toxic messes, not just “remediate” the land for development by paving it over or digging up the toxins and dumping them elsewhere.

When we turn this contaminated “brownfield” into a “greenfield” through interdisciplinary, community collaborative teams researching, implementing and teaching bio, myco, and phyto remediation, non-chemical non-native plant removal, and facilitating native species polyculture rewidling, we can begin to understand how to reconcile climate change and heal the damaged spaces left behind from two centuries of “progress.”

Perhaps we can re-discover the ways we humans might live in partnership with nature. In the process, we can build our eco-literacy and our awareness of how natural systems work. This is essential to not making the same mistakes again.

Our mission is to conserve, restore, remediate and rematriate the Pine Street Barge Canal, all the while educating our community on caring for land and growing ecoliteracy.

Want to get involved?

The Barge Canal needs your voice, your action and your commitment!

  • Become a member of Friends of the Barge Canal by signing up to stay in touch with the work and find the many ways you can step into the Barge Canal.

  • Donate. We are volunteers but we work with professionals and there are expenses. We need $25,000 now to continue this work for another year.

  • Come to our bi-monthly community science sessions, do hands-on learning about non-native plant removal, and come to meetings.

  • Tell your local elected representatives (City Councilors, State Representatives, Governor) that you care about what happens at the Barge Canal.
